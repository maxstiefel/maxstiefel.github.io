Burn Probabilities

Current and future regional-scale fire regimes were summarized within sixteen “homogeneous fire regime zones” (HFRZ) throughout Canada. Models (multivariate adaptive regression splines, MARS) predicting monthly annual area burned from various climate and fire-weather related variables were developed for each HFRZ and are detailed in Boulanger et al. (2014). Then, these models were used in conjunction with future climate projections to estimate future regional-scale burn probabilities. These climate projections were obtained from three different earth system models (ESM), i.e., the CanESM2, the HadGEM3, and the MIROC-ESM, for three different anthropogenic climate forcing scenarios, namely RCP 2.6, RCP 4.5, and RCP 8.5. For each climate scenario and ESM, climate variables were summarized over three 30-yr periods i.e, 2011 – 2040, 2041 – 2070, and 2071-2100.

Burn probabilities for 2020 were obtained by combining information about vegetation properties and regional-scale projections of fire regimes. They represent the probability that pixels belonging to a given vegetation type might burn each year in a given region. Indeed, burn probabilities are influenced by what we call “bottom-up” and “top-down” factors, both of which are acting at different spatial scales. For example, bottom-up factors represent the “fuel” component, in this case, the vegetation type. These factors are mostly influencing burn probabilities at a fine spatial scale, as vegetation can be very heterogeneous over a large territory.

In the wildfire world, it was demonstrated that some vegetation types are more likely to burn than others. For instance, increasing conifer content is likely to increase local burn probabilities. In the same way, older stands are more likely to burn than young stands. On the other hand, climate variables can be considered as top-down factors. These mostly influence burn probabilities at the synoptic scale (i.e., at the regional to supra-regional scale), as climate is likely to be relatively similar over large areas. Simply put, warm and dry climate during the fire season will result in higher burn probabilities at the regional-scale than in regions experiencing wetter and colder climate conditions.

Regional projections were then downscaled to a 250-m scale in order to consider vegetation types. First, fire selectivity representing the likelihood of a given vegetation type to burn were retrieved from Bernier et al. (2016). Seven vegetation types were defined according to conifer content (≤ 25%, >25-50%, >50-75%, and >75%) and age classes (< 30 years old, 30-90 years old, > 90 years old). In this classification, old coniferous stands are the most likely to burn (2.9 times more than randomness), whereas young deciduous stands are less likely (more than 6 times less than randomness). These vegetation types were then mapped countrywide by using 250-m scale forest attribute data (Beaudoin et al. 2014) from the Canadian National Forest Inventory (available at nfi.nfis.org). Local (250-m) current and future burn probabilities were then obtained by multiplying regional burn probabilities at the HFRZ scale with the fire selectivity ratios for each vegetation type. In these maps, vegetation was held constant among time period and climate scenarios. Consequently, results can be interpreted as “what-if” scenarios, i.e., given today’s vegetation, what would be the local burn probabilities under a given climate scenario.

Downscale and Score

Burn probabilities are produced at a relatively coarse cell size of 250m. This spatial resolution is necessary to complete simulation modeling for all areas across Canada in a reasonable amount of time, and for most national-scale applications 250-m resolution is appropriate and sufficient. To be most useful to communities, however, it was necessary to downscale the national results to a finer spatial resolution. The downscaling method we developed for this project involved resampling to a finer resolution, a process we refer to as raster upsampling. We did not re-simulate wildfire hazard at finer resolution than the native national simulated resolution or use statistical downscaling methods common with broad-scale climate data. The two processes, described in detail below, instead used a series of iterative resampling steps to downscale the national 250-m BP raster to 30-m resolution and then extend BP into developed areas:

Step 1: Downscale 250m burn probability to 30m resolution

  1. Convert zero values to NA in 250-m data
  2. Two 3x3 moving-window averages at 250 m
  3. Revert remaining NA pixels back to zero at 250 m
  4. Resample to 30-m resolution (cubic convolution method)
  5. Set BP to NA for open water and snow/ice land covers at 30 m
  6. Set negative BP to zero

The ClimateCheck Wildfire Risk Rating is based on both extreme fire weather index exceedances and burn probabilities. We estimate a joint CDF of the sum of the inverse CDFs for the fire weather index and burn probability risk ratings at 250m. This requires resampling the 10km FWI risk rating to 250m.

Step 2: Estimating wildfire hazard in developed areas

  1. Classify burnable fuels
  2. Identify small (<500ha) burnable patches
  3. Save original BP values in small patches
  4. Set non-zero BP to NA in small patches
  5. Set NA BP to zero except water/snow/ice and small patches
  6. Three 510m moving-window averages at 30m res
  7. Set final BP value in small patches to the larger of their original value and the smoothed value
