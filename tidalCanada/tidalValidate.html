Prepared by: Maximilian Stiefel

Intro

High-tide coastal flooding occurs when water inundates land during the highest tides. It is a cyclically occurring phenomenon where coastal waters exceed mean higher high water (MHHW), the average height of the highest tide recorded each day. The land above MHHW is dry most of the time. MHHW has a baseline average from the most recent National Tidal Datum Epoch from 1983-2001 and locally varies by relative sea level, tidal patterns and behavior, and geomorphological features and processes, such as elevation and erosion. Similarly, high tide flooding probability is a function of local relative sea level and coastal land elevation. Although melting ice and warmer water, which takes up more space than cooler water, increase ocean volume globally, sea level rise varies locally.

Dahl et al. (2017) found an effective inundation threshold from frequent high tide flooding using a mixed-methods approach to determine when land in a coastal community becomes unusable because it floods too frequently even if it is not permanently inundated. The authors established an effective inundation threshold of 26 flood days/year through interviews with community planners and leaders. They project land usability tipping points by estimating which areas flood more frequently than the threshold for coastal communities across the US.

Methods

The high tide flood hazard model counts the number of days annually that tidal heights exceed an elevation grid cell. Inundation exceedances are estimated from a nonparametric probability density curve of daily maximum tidal heights over several decades (???). We adjust these curves with sea level rise projections to estimate the frequency of future high tide flooding. Data for this model include high resolution digital elevation models, daily tide gauge readings, tidal datums, and sea level rise projections.

We first interpolate projected relative sea level rise for each tide gauge, find the nearest tidal gauge to each elevation grid cell, and use the daily high tide distribution from the closest tidal gauge to model exceedance probabilities. The complement of the CDF transformed elevation values s the probability of exceedance, i.e., 1 – CDF(elevation). DEMs need to match the vertical datum of tidal data, e.g., in the USA we difference MHHW from the DEM. A geospatial cleanup uses region grouping to remove hazardous cells disconnected from the sea.

The daily inundation exceedance probabilities comprise the ClimateCheck High Tide Flood Risk Rating. Multiplying these probabilities by 365 gives the annual number of high tide flooding days.

CONUS

Data and modeling products come from the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) and other sources (Marcy et al. 2011; Kopp et al. 2014; Sweet et al. 2017, 2018, 2019) to estimate high tide flood hazard:

  1. NOAA Continuously Updated Digital Elevation Model (CUDEM). Horizontal resolution is ~\(5m^2\), resampled to \(10m^2\)
  2. NOAA Mean Higher High Water (MHHW) Interpolated Surface. The 16 MHHW tiles provide a 50 to 100m2 horizontal resolution.
  3. NOAA daily tidal data from nearly 100 tidal gauges to observe tidal variability measured at tide gauges.
  4. NOAA relative sea level rise projections. One-degree grid along the coasts.

Canada

  1. Copernicus Digital Elevation Model (COP-DEM) GLO-30. Horizontal resolution is \(30m^2\).
  2. GESLA-3 (Global Extreme Sea Level Analysis Version 3). Daily tidal data from stations across the globe.
  3. Candian Hydrographic Service Chart Datum (CD) for each tidal station to vertically transform tidal heights to DEM vertical datum.
  4. Geological Survey of Canada relative sea level rise projections. Canadian standard CD is Lower Low Water Large Tide (LLWLT).
